Part 1

Why learning AI alone often stalls

If you want to build with AI, it is easy to get lost in endless information. The constant stream of new tools and ideas can make it harder to stay focused long enough to build something.

I just opened my X and there's already one more shiny project I need to check out

What happens

You see a new tool, resource, or idea and it grabs your attention, distracting you from your original focus.

Result

Instead of moving forward with your own project, you are pulled toward one more exciting thing.

After a few scrolls, I already feel behind

What happens

You keep seeing people talk as about new "must-try" tools and resources. It's hard to tell what is useful and what is just competing for your attention.

Result

You try to keep up (Hello, FOMO!), but it only makes you feel lost

You forget about your own project

What happens

You set out to build one thing, but new tools and ideas split your attention until your own project stalls.

Result

You lose momentum because of that constant content switching.

Or can't decide what to build

What happens

Because of the context overload, learning AI feels like drinking from a firehose. It's too much to choose from.

Result

You get stuck on the first step: deciding what to build.

Lack of content is not the problem

"Content is not the problem. The problem is accountability. I already have more than enough courses, projects, and tutorials to learn from. What is harder is staying consistent and actually moving things forward." - One of the AI Shipping Labs members

Part 2

Common ways people get stuck

If you have tried to learn and build on your own, you will probably recognize at least one of these situations.

1. You keep pushing your project to "next weekend"

Example

You're in a loop: you keep telling yourself next weekend will be different, but it never is.

Situation

Life gets in the way, work runs late, you're tired, and the project gets postponed again. You don't touch it, and it remains just an idea.

2. You do not know where to start

Example

One goal quickly turns into many parallel questions about roadmaps, tools, projects, internships, and what to do in what order.

Situation

You open one roadmap, then another, then a thread with tool recommendations, etc. You end up with ten tabs open and no clear sense of what to focus on first.

3. You get stuck in tutorial hell

Example

Tutorials feel clear while you are following them, but that confidence disappears when you have to decide things on your own.

Situation

You try to build your own version, but the uncertainty starts.

4. You loose momentum when things get messy

Example

You feel lack of scope, direction and project management and lose motivation when faced with errors and blockers.

Situation

You get something working and you're excited, but at some point the project is face a problem you don't know how to solve. You are stuck in the frustrating middle.

Mentoring requests

Alexey gets mentoring requests all the time.

Mentoring is hard to scale, because people need regular support, accountability and structure to keep moving.

Part 3

What AI Shipping Labs is

AI Shipping Labs is a community for people who want to keep building AI projects, not just read about them. The idea is to give people enough structure, feedback, and peer support to make steady progress.

aishippinglabs.com

How the community works

  • You learn by doing.
  • You move beyond tutorials and prototypes to building reliable systems.
  • You collaborate with others: group learning, building sessions, and accountability circles.
  • You develop better judgment through exposure to real trade-offs and feedback from peers and more experienced builders.

Part 4

What happens inside

Slack workspace

When you join, you get access to the Slack workspace, where the community works together. It's the shared place for questions, progress updates, and day-to-day interaction.

How the community works

The community is built around a few practical formats.

1

Accountability circles

Structure, planning, and regular check-ins around your own work.

2

Group learning

Members share useful takeaways from the topics they research and tools they test.

3

Building sessions

Live sessions where we work through real implementation questions.

4

Trend breakdowns

A calmer way to stay informed without chasing every new trend.

5

Career support

Feedback on resumes, LinkedIn, interviews, and next steps.

1. Accountability circles

  • You select a project to work on and join a sprint with a defined time frame and clear goals.
  • We meet for regular live check-ins, similar to stand-ups.
  • You share progress, blockers, and what you plan to do next.
  • You get feedback on your project and how to improve it.

2. Group learning

Members ask for more practical patterns for production work. One person's question can become a useful learning resource for many others.

  • You can select a topic to research or a tool to test and join a group learning session.
  • You share your findings with the rest of the community.
  • Over time, this builds into an internal knowledge base of takeaways and tools tested by the community.

3. Building sessions

Live sessions where we tackle real implementation questions together, lasting 1.5 to 2 hours and held once or twice a month. Members can suggest or vote on topics.


  • Sessions may be based on member requests or real-world problems from Alexey's own projects.
  • Focus varies: tools, architecture choices, or practical implementation trade-offs.

Example: OpenClaw session candidate

Member request

A member request can become a building session for the whole community. One person brings a specific implementation question, but the discussion is relevant for many people building with AI.

Example: LiteHive problem-solving

Alexey's project

Another format is when Alexey brings a real problem from his own project and works through it with the community. That gives people a chance to watch how someone more experienced reasons through a messy, real-world problem.

4. Keeping up without the noise

Member request

This is another recurring need: people want help understanding what matters without getting buried under constant updates. What they are really asking for is help filtering, explaining, and prioritizing what is worth attention.

5. Career support

We help with the career side of things: how to position yourself, how to present your experience, and how to make better decisions about next steps.

  • You can get resume and LinkedIn feedback from people who have been through the hiring process.
  • We help with personal branding and online presence.
  • Because the community is private, you can talk more openly about interviews, offers, salary discussions, and doubts.

Part 5

Why join as an early member

Because the community is still small, there is more room to understand each person's situation properly and adjust the experience as we learn what people need.

Personal onboarding

  • We start by understanding your situation, goals, and level, either directly with Alexey or asynchronously with Valeriia.
  • Alexey then shapes a focused plan for your next steps.
  • You join a sprint with a set timeframe and clear goals.
  • There are regular live check-ins where you share progress and blockers.

What is different right now

More time to understand each person's situation

Because the group is still small, there is still room to understand what someone is building, where they are stuck, and what support would help.

More room for direct interaction

It is easier to pay attention to individual questions and goals when there are still relatively few people in the community.

Member feedback still changes the shape of things

Feedback from early members can still directly affect which formats, topics, and activities we decide to prioritize next.

Part 6

How the membership options are structured

Most of what we described so far sits inside the Main tier, because that is where the shared community layer actually happens.

The three tiers

Basic

Written materials, tutorials, and curated resources for people who prefer self-directed learning.

Main

Community access, accountability, and live activities for people who want the full shared experience.

Premium

Everything in Main, plus mini-courses and guaranteed feedback for people who want a more guided path (especially when the community gets larger).

Basic tier

For people who prefer self-directed learning. It provides access to written materials, but no community access.

  • Hands-on tutorials with code examples.
  • Curated breakdowns of new AI tools and workflows.
  • Research notes and curated posts you might have missed.

Premium tier

For people who want a more structured layer of learning alongside the live formats. It adds access to a set of focused mini-courses and a guaranteed feedback on your resume, LinkedIn, posts, and projects.

Current mini-course ideas:

  • Python for AI Engineering
  • Spec-Driven Development for AI
  • Refactoring AI Slop

Part 7

How AI Shipping Labs relates to DataTalks.Club

How the AI Shipping Labs idea was developed

1

AI Engineering Buildcamp on Maven

Students wanted more structure and continuity than a single cohort could provide.

2

Personal newsletter

It became a place to share daily learning, experiments, and practical observations.

3

A smaller group to go deeper

Live sessions, collaboration, and shared problem-solving gradually made sense as their own community.

Over time, these pieces started fitting together naturally. It was not originally planned as a replacement for anything else.

DataTalks.Club is not going away

  • All Zoomcamps continue. Free education stays free.
  • Just like the Maven Buildcamp did not replace LLM Zoomcamp, this is not meant to replace DataTalks.Club.
  • AI Shipping Labs adds a smaller and more involved layer of support, but it does not replace the free educational work that already exists.
