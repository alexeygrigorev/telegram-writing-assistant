Part 1

Why learning AI alone often stalls

If you want to build with AI, you probably do not have a shortage of things to learn. The problem is that more information does not automatically make progress easier.

Main point

Very often, the flood of new tools and ideas makes it harder to stay focused long enough to actually build something.

There is always one more thing that feels important

What happens

You see a new tool, a new benchmark, a new workflow, and it immediately feels like something you should pay attention to. Even if you were trying to stay focused, your attention gets pulled away again.

Result

So instead of moving forward with your own project, you are pulled toward one more thing that feels urgent.

After a while, it becomes hard to tell what actually matters

What happens

When you keep seeing people talk as if some new tool or resource is essential, it becomes harder for you to tell what is genuinely useful and what is just competing for your attention.

Result

So you can spend a lot of time trying to keep up without getting much clearer about what to do next.

And your own project starts losing to everything else

What happens

You start with one thing you wanted to build. Then you see another idea, another approach, another tool that also seems worth exploring. Gradually, your attention gets split, and your own work stops moving.

Result

So your project loses momentum, not because you stopped caring, but because it became too easy to keep switching direction.

So even choosing a project can start to feel overwhelming

What happens

People often describe learning AI as drinking from a firehose. By the time you try to think of a project to build, you may already feel lost and overloaded.

Result

So people often get stuck before they even choose a project or take the first concrete step.

Part 2

How this usually shows up

If you have tried to learn and build on your own, you will probably recognize at least one of these patterns.

1. Your project keeps getting pushed to "next weekend"

Example

This post captures a familiar loop: you keep promising yourself that next weekend will be different, but the same pattern repeats again.

Situation

You keep telling yourself you will finally work on it this weekend. Then work runs late, life gets in the way, you are tired, and the project gets postponed again. After a while, it becomes something you care about, but rarely touch in practice.

2. You do not know what to learn first

Example

In the screenshot, one goal quickly turns into many parallel questions about roadmaps, tools, projects, internships, and what to do in what order.

Situation

You open one roadmap, then another, then a thread with tool recommendations, then a course outline. Everything sounds useful. Instead of starting, you end up with ten tabs open and no clear sense of what to focus on first.

3. Tutorials make sense until you try to do it on your own

Example

People even have a name for this: tutorial hell. The screenshot points to a common experience: tutorials feel clear while you are following them, but that confidence disappears when you have to decide things on your own.

Situation

When you try to build your own version, the uncertainty starts: which stack to use, how to structure it, what to do differently, and why your setup does not behave the same way as the demo.

4. Once something breaks, it is easy to lose momentum

Example

This is the quieter stage where many projects stall: not at the beginning, but later, when the work becomes messier, slower, and harder to reason about.

Situation

You get something working, but then the outputs are weak, the code becomes messy, or one part stops making sense. You are stuck in the frustrating middle, and that is where many projects quietly stop.

So usually the problem is not lack of content

Main point

You already have plenty to read and watch. What is usually missing is a way to keep going: a clearer next step, some outside accountability, and help when the project becomes confusing or messy.

What one member said

"Content is not the problem. The problem is accountability. I already have more than enough courses, projects, and tutorials to learn from. What is harder is staying consistent and actually moving things forward."

One of the AI Shipping Labs members

That is why simple mentoring advice is usually not enough

What happens

Alexey gets mentoring requests all the time. But in many cases, what people need is not just one good piece of advice.

Main point

They need a structure that helps them keep moving after the conversation ends.

Part 3

What AI Shipping Labs is

AI Shipping Labs is a community for people who want to keep building AI projects, not just read about them. The idea is to give people enough structure, feedback, and peer support to make steady progress.

aishippinglabs.com

In practice, it is meant to help with three things

Projects

Choose something concrete to build

Instead of staying in research mode, you pick a real idea and start turning it into something usable.

Progress

Keep working on it consistently

Progress is easier when there is a plan, a deadline, and other people expecting updates, so the project keeps moving.

People

Learn alongside other builders

You improve faster when you can compare approaches, talk through problems, and learn from other people making similar decisions.

How the community is supposed to work

Learn through real work

The point is not just to watch or read. Most activities are built around making something, testing it, and getting feedback on it.

Move beyond demos

The focus is on what holds up outside a tutorial, so people can move from rough prototypes to more reliable systems.

Do it with other people

Projects, hackathons, and group problem-solving make the work less isolating and make progress easier to sustain.

Develop better judgment

Good instincts come from repeated exposure to real trade-offs, plus feedback from peers and more experienced builders.

Part 4

What happens inside

What changes when you join

You are no longer trying to figure everything out on your own. Instead of learning in isolation, you get a working environment where it is easier to stay focused, ask questions, and keep your project moving.

The goal is not to give you more noise. It is to make progress easier to sustain.

Your working space from day one

Slack workspace

When you join, you get immediate access to the Slack workspace, where the community actually works together.

What people use it for

This gives people one shared place to ask questions, share progress, and stay close to what others are building.

How the community works

The community is built around a few practical formats. Some are focused on helping you keep moving on your own project. Others are there to help you learn from what other members are building and struggling with.

1

Accountability circles

Structure, planning, and regular check-ins around your own work.

2

Group learning

Members share useful takeaways from the tools and approaches they test.

3

Building sessions

Live sessions centered on real implementation questions and trade-offs.

4

Trend breakdowns

A calmer way to stay informed without trying to follow everything.

5

Career support

Feedback on positioning, resumes, LinkedIn, interviews, and next steps.

1. Accountability circles

What people get

This is for people who do not just want inspiration, but a structure that helps them keep going.

In practice

A personal plan, clear deadlines, regular check-ins, and a group that knows what you are working on.

How it works

  • When you join, we first try to understand your situation, goals, and current level.
  • This can happen directly with Alexey or asynchronously with Valeriia's help.
  • Based on that, Alexey helps shape a personal plan for what to focus on next.
  • Members who want this kind of structure join a sprint with a defined time frame and clear goals.
  • We then meet for regular live check-ins, similar to stand-ups.
  • You share what you worked on, where you got stuck, and what you plan to do next.
  • The point is not pressure for the sake of pressure. It is to make it harder for your project to quietly disappear from your week.

2. Group learning

Why this exists

A lot of useful learning happens while people are building their own projects. The idea here is not to let that stay private and disappear.

In practice

Research becomes more useful when people turn it into something concrete that others can reuse.

How it works

  • As part of their project work, members often research a tool, test an approach, compare options, or try to understand how something works.
  • Instead of keeping that work to themselves, they can share short takeaways with the rest of the community.
  • This helps other people who are running into similar questions.
  • It also reinforces accountability, because turning your learning into something shareable forces you to make it more concrete.
  • Over time, this builds an internal library of experiments, lessons, and examples grounded in real use cases.

Member request

Why this works as a group format

One person’s question can become a useful learning resource for many other people in the community.

3. Building sessions

What the sessions are for

These are live sessions where we work through real implementation questions together. The goal is not to watch slides. The goal is to look at an actual problem and think through it properly.

Format

Usually 1.5 to 2 hours, once or twice a month. Members can suggest and vote on topics.

What happens there

  • Some sessions come directly from member requests.
  • Someone may want to understand a specific tool, architecture choice, or implementation trade-off.
  • Other sessions can come from Alexey’s own projects, where he brings a real problem and shows how he approaches it.
  • The useful part is not just the final answer, but seeing how an experienced builder reasons through ambiguity, blockers, and decisions.
  • Many of these topics are highly relevant beyond one person’s project, which is why they work well as shared sessions.

Example: OpenClaw session candidate

Member request

A concrete member request can easily become a useful building session for the whole community.

Why it can help other people too

One person brings a specific implementation question, but the discussion is often useful for many people building with AI.

Another example is when Alexey brings a real problem from his own project and works through it with the community.

What people can learn from it

That gives people a chance to watch how someone more experienced reasons through a messy, real-world problem.

What it helps with

  • This reduces the feeling that you have to constantly keep up on your own.

5. Career support

Why this is part of the community

The community is not only about project work. A lot of people also need help with the career side of things: how to position themselves, how to present their experience, and how to make better decisions about next steps.

In practice

Some of the most important career questions are also the hardest ones to ask in public.

What people can ask about

  • You can get resume and LinkedIn feedback from people who understand hiring and know what tends to work.
  • There is also room for personal branding support: learning in public, posting, and building a stronger online presence.
  • Because the community is private, people can talk more openly about interviews, offers, salary discussions, and career doubts.
  • That matters because many of these questions are hard to ask publicly, even though they affect major decisions.

Part 6

How the membership options are structured

Most of what we described so far sits inside the Main tier, because that is where the shared community layer actually happens.

Main is where most of the shared activity happens

Basic

Mostly for self-directed learning

  • Hands-on tutorials with code examples.
  • Curated breakdowns of new AI tools and workflows.
  • Behind-the-scenes access to ongoing research and experiments.
  • A curated collection of valuable social posts you might have missed.

Main

For people who want the shared community experience

  • Community access.
  • Accountability circles.
  • Building sessions and group learning.
  • Trend breakdowns, career support, and private discussions.

Premium

For people who want extra structure and guaranteed feedback

  • Everything in Main.
  • Access to mini-courses on specialized topics.
  • Guaranteed feedback on your resume, LinkedIn, social media, and projects.
  • More structured guidance for people who want extra support.

What Premium adds

What is included beyond Main

Premium adds a set of focused mini-courses on top of the community itself, for people who want a more structured layer of learning alongside the live formats.

Still evolving

This part is still evolving, and early members can still influence which mini-courses get built next.

Current examples

  • Python for AI Engineering.
  • Spec-Driven Development for AI.
  • Refactoring AI Slop.

A simple way to think about the three tiers

Basic

Written materials, tutorials, and curated resources for people who prefer self-directed learning.

Main

Community access, accountability, and live activities for people who want the full shared experience.

Premium

Everything in Main, plus mini-courses and guaranteed feedback for people who want a more guided path.

Part 7

How this relates to DataTalks.Club

How it developed

1

AI Engineering Buildcamp on Maven

Students wanted more structure and continuity than a single cohort could provide.

2

Personal newsletter

It became a place to share daily learning, experiments, and practical observations.

3

A smaller group to go deeper

Live sessions, collaboration, and shared problem-solving gradually made sense as their own community.

Bottom line

Over time, these pieces started fitting together naturally. It was not originally planned as a replacement for anything else.

DataTalks.Club is not going away

What stays the same

All Zoomcamps continue. Free education stays free.

What AI Shipping Labs adds

AI Shipping Labs adds a smaller and more involved layer of support. It does not replace the free educational work that already exists.

It serves a different purpose

A useful comparison

Just like the Maven Buildcamp did not replace LLM Zoomcamp, this is not meant to replace DataTalks.Club.

What this is for

It exists for people who want a smaller setting, more regular interaction, and the kind of support that is difficult to provide sustainably inside a large free community.
