# Interview Process Webinar Q&A

Today there was a webinar about the interview process - what they actually ask at interviews. I shared my research and talked about what gets asked. I answered questions during the webinar itself, and as usual, some questions remained. After the webinar I answered them via voice notes[^1].

## Finding a Niche in AI Engineering

Question: What can someone do to find a niche in AI Engineering? According to branding experts, they typically recommend this but finding a niche is tough. They don't say how[^0].

It is hard to say what branding experts recommend. In my experience, it is more like the niche finds you rather than you finding the niche[^1].

When I was in university, there were things I worked on. For example, I worked on digital watermarking, and it sort of happened naturally that I got into it. I did not specifically look for this area. It was not like I had a specific niche in this area, but it was research in data science. Since I had more of an engineering background, over time I moved more into MLOps. So for people, I was more of an MLOps expert than an expert in some other area[^1].

Right now I do not know if I have a specific niche or not. So it is hard to answer. I think you can just follow your interests and they will lead you somewhere. That is what I would say. From a brand marketing perspective, this might not be the best advice, because AI engineering is still a fairly broad area[^1].

If you are not currently working, you cannot just pick a niche based on interests alone. You might find a job in a different niche and grow there. So I would say do not worry too much about it and see where life leads you[^1].

## Frontend Engineer to Senior AI Engineer After Bootcamp

Question: Can someone with 5 years of experience in software engineering (frontend) apply for a senior AI engineer role after a bootcamp?[^0]

Senior - probably not, because seniority implies that you have significant experience specifically in this area. I would not say you can become a senior right away, but mid-level is quite realistic[^2].

I have already talked about the roadmap. We have a learning path. For a frontend engineer after a bootcamp, it is quite realistic to become an AI engineer, but senior is too ambitious. I would not count on it[^2].

There might be a downgrade - if you are currently a senior, when you switch to AI engineering you will not be a senior. But that is nothing terrible. Senior is more about ownership. If you join a company and they do not hire you as a senior based on tech skills, but you take ownership of a large piece of work, you will practically be performing the senior role immediately. The company should notice and reward that accordingly[^2].

Senior engineer is first and foremost a senior engineer - the frontend specialization is secondary. But when companies look for a senior engineer, they value previous experience in that specific area[^2].

## Transitioning from Bioinformatics to AI Engineering

Question: As an MS Bioinformatics graduate with AlphaFold skills, how can I transition to AI Engineer roles requiring production/cloud ML to land my first interview?[^0]

This is the hardest transition for research-oriented roles. But I have had graduates from my course with this kind of background. When you have a research background, things like evaluation come much easier to you than to others. That is your superpower. I would build your experience around that[^3].

But you definitely need to add production experience - without it, engineering will not work. How to add it? You do projects. You take a project and build it end-to-end so that it has deployment. You figure out how to add tests - tests go without saying. How to set up CI/CD? Given that today there are many AI assistants available, you just ask the assistant to do it and try to understand everything. You ask the assistant questions - why this way and not that way? Because at interviews, if people ask you, you need to know the answer. Even if you did not do it independently, you still need to understand how it works and be able to answer questions about it[^3].

So the plan is: do 2-3 projects, interview in parallel, see what they ask. And as I said, put more emphasis on evaluation, because that is your superpower. As a researcher, this is relatively easy for you, while for engineers - say someone transitioning from frontend to AI engineering - these things are harder for them[^3].

## Dealing with Interview Failures

Question: I'm interviewing but can't close interviews. I do okay on the first-round coding test but fail as the interview progresses. Tips to manage difficult code tests?[^0]

Treat this as feedback. Think of it as a learning process. You go there, you get some feedback, you understand where you need to improve. After each interview I would do a retrospective and think about where I messed up, where things did not go well, what the reason was, and try to build a plan for how to approach it next time[^4].

You also need to think about whether this is systematic. If you failed an interview at one company, it does not mean much - it just happened that way. If you went through 3-4 and failed at the same stage, then you can clearly see a systematic pattern and start building a plan around it. But even from a single interview, you get very useful feedback - you can estimate what you need to do to pass a similar interview next time[^4].

The main thing is to keep progressing. You can even ask ChatGPT to pick specific LeetCode problems for you to practice. You set aside an hour or two every day to solve them. When solving problems, you try yourself first. If you cannot figure it out, you can ask ChatGPT for help - but you ask it to help, not to solve it for you. You say: "I am stuck here, how should I approach this?" You do not ask for a ready solution[^4].

You can also read forums on LeetCode where people discuss solutions. There are many options. You can tell ChatGPT: "I am trying to solve problems like this and I cannot, let's analyze a similar but simpler problem, give me a hint on where to start." If you are completely stuck, you solve a simpler problem first, then you can move on to solve the harder one[^4].

The key is to use AI assistants like ChatGPT not as a source of ready solutions, but as a teacher or mentor who tries to make sure you solve the problem yourself as much as possible. You will need to ask ChatGPT to behave this way - by default it will not do it on its own[^4].

## How Important Is Working Code in Interviews

Question: How important is having working code vs just working logic during interviews?[^0]

More often it is not as important as you might think. It depends on the task[^5].

If the task is complex, having working code at the end can be difficult. Let's take the case where you are not using AI assistants and writing all code yourself. If we are talking about a task like writing a web crawler - it is hard to make a working crawler in 30 minutes, especially with interview stress. So often you say "here I would implement it this way, but I don't have time to implement it now, let's just assume there is an implementation here"[^5].

If it is a take-home assignment, then yes - the code should be working, and there should be tests. But for live coding, it does not always work out[^5].

Same with tests. It is good if you can add them and they are relevant, but I would not force it. In some cases tests make sense, in others they do not. If you have a LeetCode-style problem, then tests - well, the platform usually has its own testing system, so that is tricky. But if you are writing in your own VS Code, you can totally write unit tests and check things if your project is set up for it[^5].

It all depends on circumstances. But I would say more often there is no expectation that code will be 100% working. What they really want to check is how you think[^5].

## Interview Process by Experience Level

Question: Does the interview process look the same across every experience level?[^0]

No, of course not. A deep dive for a junior will look different. System design will most likely not be there at all. Behavioral interviews will also be different[^6].

For seniors, all of this is very important. Behavioral will have a big focus. System design will be there almost always - not necessarily, but more often than not. Because from a senior, the expectation is the ability to decompose some complex system into something simpler, and then give less experienced colleagues the opportunity to work on it[^6].

At the staff level and above, there might be even more system design rounds. It all depends. At junior level, if you do not have much experience, there will be more LeetCode. If you are already a senior, there might be less LeetCode and more serious coding and system design[^6].

## PM Transitioning to Engineering

Question: How do we understand the best way to answer these questions? I am a PM who wants to get into engineering roles. Will be helpful to understand how to prep[^0].

The best way, as I said, is to work on specific projects. You implement them and through that you will know the right answers. Right now you look at these questions and think "how will I ever be able to answer all of this?" In practice, with experience it comes. The more projects you do, the easier it becomes to answer, including these interview questions. Just by doing two or three AI engineering projects, you will already know the answer to half the questions[^8].

PM is an interesting case. Right now the PM skill is very important for working with AI assistants. As an engineer, I have to be a PM when I set tasks for AI. And I do not always do it well, because while I have worked with PMs and roughly understand the process, it is not my core skill. It is hard. But PMs already have this skill, and if they can learn to set tasks for Claude Code the way they need to - that is, they already know how to do this and ask it to create GitHub issues[^8].

PM skills are very relevant now for these "vibe coders" - people who rely entirely on AI for implementation. If you can write a good specification, good acceptance criteria, and good test scenarios, then systems like Claude Code can execute all of this very well. So you have a superpower - the ability to set tasks so they are maximally unambiguous. Many engineers do not have this skill[^8].

Similar to the advice I already gave to someone from a genomics background who can do certain things - you might not know how to implement it, but you know what it should look like, how it should work, and what the acceptance criteria are. You know what the end result should be, not how to get there. And that is where the superpower lies. You can start from this entry point into engineering - setting tasks for agents so they execute them, and through this gradually try to understand what the agent produced. The more you dig into it, the more you will understand[^8].

Of course, an agent can write code that you will not understand - they really do write complex code sometimes. But at least you can start understanding some things, and you can do small practice exercises. Where there is not too much code, try to gradually understand more and more[^8].
